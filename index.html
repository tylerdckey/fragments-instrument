What this instrument is — and isn't

This borrows Snowden's SenseMaker grammar — an open prompt, then you signify your own story with triads and sliders rather than interpreting it — and bends it to a single life. That bend has a cost. With one person signifying, self-interpretation can't correct your bias the way it does across a crowd. The repair is a real person: have someone you trust place a few of your fragments in their own words first, then look at where their reading diverges from yours.

No schedule, no streak, no quota. Capture when a moment grabs you — five some days, none on others; zero isn't a lapse. Form stays the same every time (that's the calm part); when and how often is entirely yours. This is externalised memory, an accommodation — you don't have to hold the shape of your life in working memory; the instrument holds it and shows you later.

The Read tab is gated behind a written prediction — best done when a pile has built up or you feel foggy, not daily. You're hoping to be surprised; if every read confirms your guess, the prompt is too leading. Agency and service are triads, not sliders, because each holds three real things that don't reduce to a line. The ★ this matters flag is your own signal, surfaced on its own. The Read tab also shows a vector — which way the good moments are drifting — as a direction to notice, never a score to chase. The grammar is Snowden's; treating your own moments as the population, the prediction gate, the salience flag, the vector read, and the skip-friendly framing are a synthesis on top of his method.
